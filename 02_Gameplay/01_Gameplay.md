# The Game Mechanics

## Setting the Scene

Scenes are primarily a narrative tool for the orator to structure adventures. Scenes fall into three broad states, with some activities overlapping. For example, depending on context, traveling could be considered an exploration or downtime activity.

The gamemaster sets each scene and manages the transition from one to the next, often as part of the adventure or plot, or in response to player cues. Transitions can occur seamlessly or dramatically, depending on the situation, thereby maintaining the narrative's dynamic flow.

### Exploration Scenes

 Exploration scenes are primarily for investigation and discovery, with emphasis on delving, examining, problem-solving, searching, and traveling. Exploration can be used by the orator for worldbuilding and to raise anticipation during an adventure.
***Narration.*** Descriptive settings, environmental interactions, player choices, and building anticipation.
***Pacing.*** Variable, can range from slow and methodical to fast and dangerous.
***Time.*** Turns in adventuring scenes represent variable amounts of time, ranging from minutes for a quick investigation to hours for traveling between locations or setting up camp.
***Breaks.*** Characters can take short breaks within an adventuring scene, often lasting only minutes, usually between encounters. These breaks typically allow for healing and recovery from certain conditions (see Recovery and/or Conditions).

### Downtime Scenes

Downtime scenes are primarily for recovery and development.

***Emphasis.*** Activities that occur while not adventuring, like carousing, crafting, research, and training.
***Narration.*** Character development, character goals, worldbuilding, and building relationships between characters and the setting.
***Pacing.*** Typically slow, focused on long-term character development.
***Time.*** Turns in downtime scenes typically represent days, weeks, or even more extended periods.
***Rests.*** Characters can rest during a downtime scene for several benefits. Rests typically occur over an extended period (e.g., an overnight rest) and provide significant rewards, including `full recovery (see Recovery)`.

### Encounter Scenes

Encounter scenes are primarily for conflict and interaction.

***Emphasis.*** Activities like conflict or interacting with characters, monsters, and other beings.
***Narration.*** Dynamic action, character choices under pressure, NPC personalities, and immediate consequences.
***Pacing.*** Typically fast, with a sense of urgency.
***Time.*** Turns in encounter scenes are typically represented in "moments," usually mere seconds.

>**Transition to Structured Turns**
>
>When a scene transitions to an encounter, the gameplay often shifts to a more structured turn-taking format, where the flow of actions is dictated by `preparedness, range, and momentum (see Combat).` However, not all encounters require this structured approach. GMs should use their discretion to determine when such a format is necessary. This transition will likely occur during a chase, social conflict, negotiation, or combat.

#### Advantage / Disadvantage Dice

When the gamemaster deems it appropriate, characters may gain an advantage or disadvantage based on the action or situation. These modifiers are usually applied to the check but may also apply to any single die roll or even multiple dice (dice pools).

> **Cancelling Advantage/Disadvantage**
>
> It's essential to note that advantage dice and disadvantage dice cancel each other out equally until only one side remains, which is then applied to the check normally.

***Advantage (ADV)*** improves the odds by adding an extra dice to the roll and using the best result, discarding the worst. Under these rules, multiple advantage dice stack. For percentile dice, advantage allows the player to swap the dice positions (ones and tens) *AFTER* the roll to achieve the best outcome. During character creation, a 4d6 dice pool with advantage (dropping the lowest die) is used for `Prime Attributes`.

***Disadvantage (DIS)*** worsens the odds by adding an extra dice to the roll and using the worst result, discarding the best. Under these rules, multiple disadvantage dice stack. For percentile dice, disadvantage forces the player to swap the dice positions (ones and tens) *AFTER* the roll to achieve the worst outcome. During character creation, a 4d6 dice pool with disadvantage (dropping the highest die) is used for `Slighted Attributes`.
