# Playing the Game

A tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG or RPG) is a collaborative storytelling experience in which players create and share memorable adventures. By bringing together two or more players around a physical or virtual tabletop (VTT), these games enable participants to assume the roles of heroes in a fictional setting, much like the heroes of your favorite novel, movie, or TV series. The participants build a shared narrative that fosters imagination, teamwork, fellowship, and camaraderie.

## The Game Basics

*Unsafe Engine* is a tabletop adventure ruleset. More specifically, a rules-lite, player-facing, 3d6 roll-under, role-playing game designed for storytelling, danger, action flow, player agency, and cenematics; and largely a return to theatre-of-the-mind gameplay, but with surprising detail and tactical options.

*Unsafe Engine* is written in an old-school renaissance (OSR) style, so it plays fast and loose with less emphasis on rules and more on rulings, also known as rules as intended. Although maps and miniatures can be used, most game aids are minimal. However, unlike other lite role-playing games, the mechanics are subtly clever, with many built-in features and systems that automatically scale the game.

*Unsafe Engine* also allows for greater choices, more hero options, detailed equipment, and many supplemental rules to augment and customize gameplay. Whether you're looking for streamlined mechanics or a more narrative-focused experience, this framework offers a flexible ruleset that adapts to your style of play.

*Unsafe Engine* is designed to serve as a creative guide, enabling you to add your own flavors and houserules to expand the game as it supports third-party content through our `game license`. However, unlike other open RPGs, we have established `directives` to add new content and systems to the core rules.

> **Theatre-of-the-mind** is at the heart of *Unsafe Engine*, a style of play in which everything in-game is wholly imagined and takes place in the players' minds. Here, the group can experience interactive adventures through the back-and-forth storytelling between the gamemaster and the players. Many modern rulesets rely on overly complex rules that get in the way, bogging down the game and interfering with its flow.
>
> Although handouts, maps, and basic miniature placement can be used, theatre-of-the-mind allows gaming groups to focus more on storylines, descriptions, characterizations, and the fine details other RPGs leave behind. In addition, the gamemaster will spend less time preparing the tools of the trade for each adventure. Many consider this mind’s eye approach the quintessential gaming style.

## The Game Roles

A tabletop role-playing game typically involves a group of players, usually ranging from 2 to 8, with one player serving as the gamemaster.

**The players** role-play the heroes, the protagonists who strive to survive and thrive in the adventures. Players make decisions for their heroes, shaping the story through their choices and rolling dice to determine the performance of their heroes' actions.

**The Gamemaster** (GM) directs the flow of the adventure and assumes the roles of all characters, particularly the antagonists that oppose the heroes. These characters include all `monsters, bosses, minions, bystanders, samaritans, and the icons`. The gamemaster's job is to remain impartial and run the adventures by narrating the story, interpreting the rules, and directing the game's overall drift.

> **Dice rolls** are rarely made by the gamemaster. Instead, the players not only roll to resolve their heroes' actions, but also make reaction rolls to avoid damage and unwanted effects against their heroes. This process enables the gamemaster to focus on adjudicating the rules and the narrative elements, ensuring the adventure remains engaging and enjoyable for everyone involved.

## The Game Modes

*Unsafe Engine* offers several approaches to gameplay.

**Group play** is the primary play mode, featuring a group of players and a gamemaster, and also includes one-on-one sessions. Except where noted, this ruleset assumes this mode.

**Co-op play** allows a group of players to play without an gamemaster. The players collaborate to tell the story, using as much or as little of the `oracle` as needed to guide the narrative.

**Solo play** is when a single player assumes the roles of both player and gamemaster, creatively using the `oracle` and various tables to help guide the narrative.

## The Game Sessions

**The adventures** are governed and often created by the gamemaster. Adventures are divided into scenes by the gamemaster. Short adventures, referred to as one-shots or side quests, can take anywhere from hours to days to complete, some spanning multiple sessions. Longer adventures can last weeks or months. A longer, more traditional adventure is a self-contained quest with a clear goal, though it can also be episodic, with plots unfolding across multiple scenarios. Adventures can span various locations, from cities to dungeons, and even traverse countless worlds, planes, dimensions, and timelines, each offering unique challenges. Beyond being fun to play, adventures drive hero development and growth, improving the core features of each hero, and much more.

**The campaigns** are a series of interconnected adventures, usually tied together by an overarching plot or hook that moves the heroes toward an ultimate goal, often culminating in a final confrontation. Campaigns can last months or years, with players pausing and revisiting them over many sessions, and can even span generations of heroes. Campaign worlds may also be run and developed by multiple gamemasters, who can alternate between adventures or collaborate in creating an ongoing, immersive setting.

## The Game Materials

A few supplies are needed to use this ruleset, which can be physical, digital, or a combination of both. Typically, a playgroup will draw maps, take notes, and track their heroes' statistics; see Appendix XX and our official website for various game aids, sheets, forms, and more. The gamemaster should outline any material requirements or restrictions before the game begins, such as whether digital or printed tools are preferred. At a minimum, each player will need a hero sheet and a set of six 6-sided dice (6d6 or 6d).

## The Game Etiquette

Gaming is a social contract between the players and a gamemaster, but it's everyone's job to create an atmosphere of fun. Together, the gamemaster and the players co-write the ongoing storyline from each hero's perspective. For all this to function as intended, a few player guidelines should be observed:

**Don't min-max your hero** by trying to exploit the game rules.

**Don't use player knowledge** that your hero wouldn't possess.

**Play your hero**, not the hero sheet; focus on the roleplaying.

**Be prepared** and understand what your hero will do on your next turn.

**Participate in the game** and engage with your fellow players and the gamemaster.

**Take good notes** to avoid being surprised and from looking up rules too often.

**Enjoy yourself** and the time spent with friends, caffeine, and carbohydrates.

Also, the gamemaster will often make judgment calls to adjust the flow and pace of the game, but should never railroad the players; rather, the gamemaster should allow the game to evolve naturally, moment by moment, from the creativity of everyone involved, and from each toss of the dice.

> **The gamemaster's Mandate**. It's worth noting that the gamemaster has the administrative role and, as such, has the final say over ANY rule or in-game situation. If the gamemaster wishes to make a judgment call, change, or houserule anything in the game on the spot, that's the gamemaster's purview. However, it's always best to discuss any homebrewed changes beforehand and set expectations; a good session zero, typically used to create heroes, can also serve this purpose. Always respect each other and pause the game when issues arise. Discuss what is happening and adjust before proceeding. Nuff Said.

## The Game Conventions

These conventions are customarily practiced throughout this ruleset.

**Always round up** unless specified otherwise. Any division or multiplication of numbers will always round up, no matter the fraction. `In addition, the minimum result is always 1.`

**Keywords and any abbreviations** (which appear in parentheses) are used throughout this ruleset and are typically defined where they first appear in the content. `Check the index to clarify whether a keyword is used but not immediately defined.` 

**Rulings, not rules**, favor the gamemaster; making good judgment calls is preferred rather than relying on overly specific protocols. If a rule cannot be found quickly, make the wisest decision possible to keep the game flowing. Look it up later.

**Specific beats general** takes precedence in the arbitration of the rules. Some rules may break or contradict others. In such cases, more specific rules usually override general rules, per the gamemaster.
